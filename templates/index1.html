About

Just a little about our project.

Model

We used YOLOv8, a ViT (Vision Transformer) for creating a trained object detection model. YOLOv8 works as a backbone for R-CNN models, consistent with many other object detection models. The Python scripting was based off of a basic model that RoboFlow provided as a standard object detection model. For our model, we trained it off of Google Colab, where we were able to collect data on the mAP (Mean Average Precision), loss rate, and the confusion matrix detailing what we could improve.

Data

We found a large database of X Ray images that were perfect for our project. But unfortunately they were annotated in an unroboflow friendly format. We ended up having to create a script to convert them to a different format. We didn't have enough images of shuriken and knives so we had to utilize some images from the dataset that were unannotated entirely in order to end up with usable data.

Problems

We faced many problems in developing our software. In roboflow, exists a feature that allows you to upload pre existing image annotations, but our annotations were not in the format that roboflow accepts, so we had to make a program to convert our existing annotations into the correct .json format. Another problem had to do with our project name “Outsiden’t”. Because of the apostrophe, when we tried to import the Outsiden’t data folder into colab, many errors occured, and we had to remove the apostrophe from the name. Lastly, we noted underrepresented categories, shurikens and knives. We were able to fix this using extra data from our datasets.

Timeline

A short timeline of our project

  • Week 1

    Data Collection

    In pursuance of suitable, X-Ray images to comprise our dataset, multiple avenues of collection were tested. We began with attempting to manually select photos from google images, which was proven ultimately futile due to the minimum dataset size requirement expected for viability. Next, we attempted to utilize a webscraper to automatically collect images, but this idea was eventually scrapped due to the lack of suitable images. In the end, we found a fitting database and selected baggages as our dataset.

  • Week 2

    Training Our Model

    In accordance with training our model, a variety of techniques were experimented with. To start, we attempted to use Google Colab with preexisting models, found from various sites, however some issues arose, leading to attempting other models. The next model used was through Roboflow itself, which we were able to get working. In order to test the accuracy of this model against others, we returned to Google Colab in an attempt to test and compare the models, ultimately choosing the Roboflow model due to its higher accuracy in accordance with our goal.

  • Week 3

    Front-End Development

    During this week we worked on developing this website and integrating the backend programs into it. We were able to source a template and then edit it to our desired design, and fill in blank spaces with information about what we have done. The largest challenge was integrating the AI into the website, and making in HTML file display other more advanced code comes at the challenge of integrating other code scripts.

  • Final Project

    X-Ray Weapon Detector

    In the end, we were able to construct a product that allows for user image input, which can return the image with bounding boxes, detecting whether one of the four dangerous objects: gun, razor, knife, or shuriken, was detected in frame, with potential future plans to improve the viability of the model further or expand the number of detectable item types.
